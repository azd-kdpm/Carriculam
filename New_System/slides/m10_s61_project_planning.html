(61-2)

Gantt Chart Scheduling & Critical Path Method (CPM)

  • Project Work Breakdown Structure (WBS): Divides deployment into 5 phases: First-Fix Conduit, Cable Pulling, Second-Fix Mounting, Rack Termination, and Commissioning.
  • Critical Path Identification: Civil trenching and main riser conduit installation are on the critical path; delays here delay total project completion.
  • Resource Levelling: Allocates technician teams efficiently between cable pulling and server room rack patching.
Project Management Rule: Civil trenching and conduit installation MUST be completed before pulling Cat6/Fiber cables.
(61-3)

Cable Containment: PVC Conduits, GI Trunking & Cable Trays

  • 40% Max Conduit Fill Ratio: Maximum allowable cable fill area in PVC/GI conduits to prevent cable friction damage during pulling.
  • Galvanized Iron (GI) Trunking: Required in riser shafts and industrial basements for high mechanical impact protection.
  • Wire Mesh Cable Trays: Suspended above ceiling tiles for flexible, ventilated structured cable distribution.
Installation Rule: NEVER exceed 40% conduit fill capacity; leave 60% free space for heat dissipation and future cable additions.
(61-4)

Separation Distances: Low-Voltage Data vs High-Voltage AC Power

  • Electromagnetic Interference (EMI): Running Cat6 cables parallel to 230V/400V power lines induces 50Hz AC noise into data pairs.
  • Minimum 300mm (12 Inches) Separation: Maintain at least 300mm distance between parallel data conduits and mains electrical cables.
  • 90-Degree Perpendicular Crossing: If data cables must cross AC power cables, cross them strictly at a 90° right angle to minimize EMI.
EMI Prevention: Always maintain 300mm separation between data and power lines; cross at 90 degrees if necessary.
(61-5)

Server Rack & Equipment Room Layout Standards

  • 19-Inch Rack Unit (1U = 1.75 Inches): Standardized vertical rack spacing for patch panels, PoE switches, and NVR servers.
  • Hot-Aisle / Cold-Aisle Airflow: Cool air enters server front; hot exhaust air vents out the back to prevent equipment overheating.
  • Cable Dressing & D-Rings: Neat Velcro cable ties and vertical D-ring managers keep rack wiring clean and serviceable.
Rack Engineering: Use Velcro straps instead of plastic zip ties to prevent crushing delicate Cat6 copper conductors.
(61-6)

Earthing, Grounding & Lightning Surge Protection Standards

  • Single-Point Grounding Busbar (TMGB): All outdoor camera metal housings, rack frames, and SPDs connected to main building earth.
  • Earth Resistance Benchmark (< 5 Ohms): Verify earthing pit resistance with a 3-point earth tester before commissioning outdoor cameras.
  • Equipotential Bonding: Prevents high voltage potential differences between separate metal server racks during lightning strikes.
Safety Standard: Outdoor CCTV metal poles MUST be grounded to an earth pit with resistance under 5 ohms.
(61-7)

Site Health, Safety & PPE Compliance (OSHA Standards)

  • Mandatory Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): Hard hat, safety glasses, high-visibility vest, steel-toe boots, and insulated gloves.
  • Working at Heights Safety (Harness & Scaffolding): Full-body safety harness with dual lanyards required when working above 2 meters.
  • Lockout / Tagout (LOTO) Procedure: De-energize and lock electrical breakers before opening live electrical junction boxes.
Site Safety Rule: ALWAYS inspect ladders, scaffolding, and full-body harnesses before working at heights above 2 meters.
(61-8)

Quality Assurance (QA) Inspection & Cable Labeling Standards

  • ANSI/TIA-606-B Labeling Standard: Self-laminating wrap-around cable labels on both ends of every cable (e.g. RACK-A/PP-01/PORT-12).
  • Fluke Cable Certification Audit: Wiremap, length, attenuation, and NEXT crosstalk testing using Fluke DSX-8000 CableAnalyzer.
  • As-Built Drawing Updates: Marking exact physical camera positions and conduit routes on CAD floorplans after installation.
Labeling Rule: Every cable MUST be labeled on both ends using TIA-606-B standards for instant identification.
(61-9)

Troubleshooting Cable Pulling Damage & Structural Obstacles

  • Excessive Pulling Tension (> 110N): Stretching Cat6 cables deforms internal twist ratios, causing Fluke NEXT crosstalk failures; re-pull cable.
  • Sharp Conduit Bend Radius (< 4x Cable Diameter): Kinking cables at 90° sharp corners breaks internal conductors; use sweep elbows.
  • Blocked PVC Conduit Core: Use nylon fish tape with cable lubricant to clear debris and pull cables smoothly.
Field Caution: Never exceed 110N pulling tension on Cat6 copper cables to avoid ruining internal twist pairs.
