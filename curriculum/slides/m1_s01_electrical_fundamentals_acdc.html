Stage 1: Electrical Fundamentals & Wiring

(1-2)

Atomic Level Properties

  • Atomic Structure: Matter consists of atoms containing a central nucleus (protons & neutrons) orbited by electrons.
  • Free Electrons: Valency electrons in the outermost orbit can break free easily, creating current flow in conductors like copper.
  • Insulators: Materials with tightly bound outer electrons (rubber, PVC) that block current flow.
Key Concept: Free electron movement in copper creates electric current.
(1-3)

Voltage & Ohm's Law

  • Voltage (V): The potential difference or electrical pressure that drives charge.
  • Current (I): The rate of flow of charge (electrons) per second through a conductor.
  • Ohm's Law: Current is directly proportional to voltage and inversely proportional to resistance.

Resistance & Heat

  • Resistance (R): Opposition to current flow that converts electrical energy into heat.
  • Joule Heating: Power lost as heat increases with higher current (P = IÂ²R).
  • Cable Sizing: Thicker wires have lower resistance, preventing dangerous overheating in long runs.
Key Takeaway: Sizing cable thickness correctly prevents voltage drop and heat damage.

AC, DC & DC Ripple Profiles

  • Alternating Current (AC): Voltage periodically reverses direction (50Hz mains power).
  • Direct Current (DC): Voltage flows in a continuous single direction (batteries and power adapters).
  • DC Ripple: Residual AC voltage noise remaining on a DC line after conversion.
Warning: Excessive DC ripple causes video lines, hum bars, and camera restarts.

(1-8)

Resistor Series & Parallel Connections

  • Series Connection: Resistors connected end-to-end add total resistance (R_total = R1 + R2).
  • Parallel Connection: Resistors connected side-by-side reduce total resistance (1/R_total = 1/R1 + 1/R2).
  • EOL Resistors: Access control and alarm zones use End-of-Line resistors for wire tamper checks.
Equation: Series: R_total = R1 + R2 | Parallel: 1/R_total = 1/R1 + 1/R2
(1-9)

Electrical Series & Parallel Circuits

  • Series Circuits: Single path for current; if one device fails, the whole circuit stops.
  • Parallel Circuits: Multiple paths for current; each device receives full supply voltage independently.
  • ELV Wiring Standard: Always wire cameras and sensors in parallel power circuits.
Standard: Security cameras must always be wired in parallel power setups.
(1-10)

Two-Way Switch Connection (Staircase)

  • Function: Controls one light fixture from two separate switch locations.
  • SPDT Switches: Uses two Single-Pole Double-Throw switches connected by traveler wires.
  • Phase Switching: Always switch the Phase (Live) wire, never the Neutral wire, for safety.
Safety Rule: Phase line must route through switch terminals, never Neutral.
(1-11)

Wire Crimping & Ferrule Standards

  • Bootlace Ferrules: Metal sleeves crimped over stranded wire ends to prevent fraying inside screw terminals.
  • Wire Stripping: Strip insulation cleanly without cutting internal copper strands.
  • Crimping Tool: Use ratchet crimpers matching the wire gauge (AWG) for a gas-tight joint.
Best Practice: Always use bootlace ferrules on stranded power wires inside panels.
